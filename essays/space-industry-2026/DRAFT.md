# Space Industry 2026: A Lay of the Land

> *Part 2 of "The Beginning of the Beginning" series*

> In 1969, it cost roughly $1.5 billion per Apollo mission (about $12 billion in 2024 dollars) to send three people to the Moon. In 2024, SpaceX launched more orbital mass than every other entity on Earth combined, at roughly $2,720 per kilogram. If Starship hits its cost targets, that drops below $100/kg. The space industry isn't approaching a tipping point. It's past one.

---

## Thesis

The space industry in 2026 is not a speculative frontier. It's a functioning economy with real revenue, real companies, and real infrastructure being deployed right now. This essay maps the landscape: where we started, where we are, what the key metrics look like, who the players are, what's on the Moon, and what frameworks (legal, economic, imaginative) are still missing. Not a prediction piece. A snapshot of the current state of play.

## Section Outline

### 1. The Cost Curve — The Only Chart That Matters
- Historical launch costs: Saturn V, Space Shuttle, Atlas V, Falcon 9, Starship (projected)
- Cost per kg to LEO across eras — this is the foundational chart
- Why cost matters more than capability: cheap access enables everything else
- SpaceX's reusability revolution: Falcon 9 booster landings (300+), Starship catch system

### 2. SpaceX — The Dominant Player
- Falcon 9: workhorse, ~90% of global orbital mass in 2024
- Starship: largest rocket ever built, fully reusable architecture, current test status
- Starlink: 6,000+ satellites, est. $6.6B revenue, enabling global broadband
- Valuation: rumored $350B, potential IPO in 2025-2026
- The vertically integrated model: launch vehicles + payload (Starlink) + ground stations
- How one company holding this much market share compares to historical precedent

### 3. The Return to the Moon
- Apollo recap: 6 landings (1969-1972), 12 humans walked on the Moon, then nothing for 50+ years
- Artemis program: NASA's return framework
  - Artemis I (2022): uncrewed, successful
  - Artemis II: crewed lunar flyby (status as of 2026)
  - Artemis III: first landing since 1972, using SpaceX Starship as lander
- CLPS (Commercial Lunar Payload Services): NASA paying private companies for lunar deliveries
  - Intuitive Machines: IM-1 (Feb 2024), first private lunar landing
  - ispace, Astrobotic: attempts and status
- China's lunar program: Chang'e missions, far-side landing (Chang'e 4), sample return (Chang'e 5)
- Chart opportunity: timeline of lunar missions (1960s → gap → 2020s resurgence)

### 4. The Ecosystem — Beyond SpaceX
- **Launch:** Blue Origin (New Glenn), Rocket Lab (Electron/Neutron), Relativity Space, ULA (Vulcan)
- **Satellites & services:** Planet Labs (Earth imaging), Spire Global (weather/maritime data)
- **In-space manufacturing:** Varda Space Industries (pharmaceutical manufacturing in microgravity)
- **Lunar landers:** Blue Origin (Blue Moon), Intuitive Machines, ispace, Astrobotic
- **Space stations:** Axiom Space (commercial modules on ISS, standalone station planned), Vast (Haven-1)
- **Space economy size:** estimated ~$469B in 2024, projections of $1T+ by 2040
- **Key metrics to track:** launches per year, cost per kg, orbital mass, revenue, employment

### 5. What's on the Moon — Resources
- **Water ice:** Confirmed at lunar poles (LCROSS 2009, Chandrayaan-1, multiple confirmations)
  - Near-term value: hydrogen + oxygen = rocket propellant; a lunar "gas station" changes deep-space economics
- **Helium-3:** Abundant in lunar regolith, potential fusion fuel (caveat: fusion power itself remains speculative)
- **Rare earth elements:** Present but extraction economics unclear
- **Regolith:** In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) for construction — building with lunar material instead of shipping from Earth
- **The key insight:** The near-term killer resource is probably water (propellant), not minerals

### 6. The Governance Gap
- **Outer Space Treaty (1967):** No nation can claim sovereignty over celestial bodies — still the foundational law
- **Moon Agreement (1979):** Attempted to declare the Moon "common heritage of mankind" — only 18 ratifications, no major space power signed
- **Artemis Accords (2020):** 43 signatories, establishes norms for lunar activity (safety zones, resource extraction, heritage preservation)
- **The missing framework:** No Homestead Act for the Moon. No Pacific Railroad Act equivalent. Property rights remain undefined.
- **The tension:** Companies are investing billions in lunar capabilities, but the legal framework for commercial activity is incomplete
- **Questions to explore:** Who decides mining rights? How are "safety zones" enforced? What happens when two countries want the same crater?

### 7. Where Are We, Really?
- Snapshot framing: if this were the railroad era, what year would it be?
- The infrastructure is being built, the cost curve is bending, the companies exist
- What doesn't exist yet: the legal framework, the economic incentive structure, the public imagination for what comes after
- This sets up Essay 3 — the parallels only work if you deeply understand both sides
